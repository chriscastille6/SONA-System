Executive Summary
The three exhibits in this supplement compare MNGT 425 students to three different
kinds of real-world samples on questions that matter for hiring decisions and statistical
reasoning. In each study, the comparison group was chosen because it represents the benchmark
readers care about: working HR executives (Exhibit A), a large contemporary sample of HR
practitioners (Exhibit B), and university faculty with formal statistics training (Exhibit C).
Students held their own in each comparison and, on most practically relevant items,
outperformed the benchmark. The figures tell the story most efficiently: each exhibit pairs a one-
page narrative with a full-page landscape figure (Figures A1, B1, and C1), and readers short on
time can scan the figures first and use the text only for context. 
What the numbers show
Exhibit A (selection beliefs). Against the Terpstra (1996) HR executive sample (N =
201), students correctly rated the unstructured interview as less effective than HR executives did
(M = 2.33 vs. 3.49, p < .001) and correctly rated performance tests / SJTs and general mental
ability as more effective (M = 4.38 vs. 3.09 and 3.71 vs. 2.89 respectively, both p < .001).
Personality ratings matched. In practical terms, students lined up with modern validity evidence
on three of four high-stakes hiring choices where HR practice has been slow to update. See
Figure A1. 
Exhibit B (selection myths). Against Fisher et al. (2021) HR practitioners (N = 453),
students averaged 75.9 % correct across 10 common myths versus the HR benchmark's 56.7 %.
The aggregate difference was statistically significant (p ≈ 2.5 × 10−5). Two myths remained
significant at the item level after Benjamini–Hochberg correction: unstructured interviews as the
most valid format (s6) and emotional intelligence as a stronger predictor than general mental
ability (s9). Students were more accurate than working HR professionals on precisely the myths
that drive costly hiring mistakes. See Figure B1.

Exhibit C (interpreting p = .01). On the six-statement Haller and Krauss (2002)
instrument, 33.3 % of students answered all six correctly, compared with 0 % of psychology
students, 10 % of faculty who do not teach statistics, and 20 % of faculty who do. A one-sided

See Figure C1. 
Why it matters
The pattern is consistent across the three exhibits: on questions with real professional
consequences, MNGT 425 students at least matched, and usually exceeded, the rates reported for
the working practitioners and instructors used as benchmarks. The course targets these specific
gaps directly, and the class outcomes track the training content. 
Appropriate caveats
Class sample sizes are small (ns of 17–21) and the students are not a random sample of
any population, so these comparisons should be read as classroom benchmarks rather than
definitive replications. Response formats and administration differ across studies (for example,
Fisher et al. allowed an Uncertain response that the class instrument did not), which makes item-
level comparisons approximate.

Exhibit A: Employee Selection Beliefs Compared With Terpstra (1996)
Overview
Students in MNGT 425 rated four personnel selection procedures for perceived
effectiveness on a 1 to 5 scale and then, as teams, chose which procedures to include in a hiring
battery. The student means were compared to the HR executive benchmark reported by Terpstra
(1996) and reproduced in Highhouse (2008, Figure 1). Before finalizing their team batteries,
students used Consensus.app deep search to gather validity evidence, which they cited alongside
Sackett et al. (2016) and Schmidt and Hunter (1998) in their memos. 
Comparison sample
The benchmark is a sample of N = 201 practicing HR executives surveyed by Terpstra
(1996) about the perceived effectiveness of commonly used selection methods. In plain terms,
this is a snapshot of what experienced HR professionals in the mid-1990s thought worked best.
Highhouse (2008) used the same numbers as the anchor for his argument that HR practice has
held onto unstructured interviews longer than validity evidence justifies. 
Data
Student ratings came from a de-identified MNGT 425 class survey collected during the
selection unit. The Terpstra means were digitized from Highhouse (2008, Figure 1, left panel).
Four procedures were matched across the two studies: unstructured interview, specific aptitude
(represented in the class survey by a performance test / situational judgment test item),
personality test, and general mental ability (represented by the cognitive ability item). 
Analysis
For each of the four procedures, a one-sample t-test compared student ratings to the
Terpstra mean. The four tests are treated independently. Significance stars in the figure follow

conventional thresholds (* p < .05, ** p < .01, *** p < .001). Final team selections are reported

Results
Student ratings were available for n = 21 respondents per procedure. Three of the four
comparisons were statistically significant at p < .001. In plain terms, the differences are large
enough that chance alone is not a credible explanation. Students rated the unstructured interview 
lower than HR executives did (M = 2.33 vs. 3.49; p < .001), which lines up with modern validity
evidence. They rated the performance test / SJT higher (M = 4.38 vs. 3.09; p < .001) and general
mental ability higher (M = 3.71 vs. 2.89; p < .001), again consistent with contemporary meta-
analytic findings. The personality test comparison was not statistically significant (M = 3.00 vs.
2.93; p = .74), meaning students and HR executives held essentially the same view of personality
testing. Table A1 reports the full set of descriptive means and one-sample tests.

Exhibit B: Selection Myths Compared With Fisher et al. (2021)
Overview
Students judged 10 common selection myths as True or False. The share of students who
correctly rejected each statement was compared to HR practitioner data reported by Fisher et al.
(2021). 
Comparison sample
The benchmark is a sample of N = 453 HR practitioners in the United States and Canada
surveyed by Fisher et al. (2021). In plain terms, this is a large, contemporary sample of working
HR professionals whose everyday decisions affect hiring. Fisher et al. built their instrument from
Rynes, Colbert, and Brown's (2002) original set of research and practice gaps, so the items
represent textbook selection myths rather than obscure trivia. 
Data
Class responses were collected through Canvas during the selection unit (n = 17), using a
forced True/False format for each of the 10 items. HR benchmark percentages were derived from
Fisher et al. (2021, Table 2), which reports % True, % False, and % Uncertain for each item.
Because the student instrument forced a binary choice, "% correct" for the HR sample is
computed as 100 − % True (i.e., % False + % Uncertain). 
Analysis
For each myth, an exact one-sided binomial test compared the student True rate to the
Fisher benchmark True rate, with the alternative hypothesis that students endorse the myth less
often than HR practitioners. Per-item p-values were adjusted across the 10 myths using the
Benjamini and Hochberg (1995) false discovery rate procedure. A second, aggregate-level test
used a one-sided Poisson test on the total count of myths endorsed across students, with the
expected rate derived from the sum of Fisher True rates.

Results
Averaged across the 10 myths, students answered correctly about 75.9 % of the time; the
HR benchmark averaged 56.7 %. The aggregate Poisson test was statistically significant (p ≈ 2.5
× 10−5). In plain terms, the gap between students and HR practitioners is too large to be a lucky
accident of the sample. 
At the item level, after BH-FDR correction for running 10 tests, two myths stayed
significant at q < .05 with students rejecting them more often than HR practitioners did: s6
(unstructured interviews are the most valid format) and s9 (emotional intelligence beats general
mental ability). In plain terms, students were clearly better on these two headline myths. On the
other eight myths, students still answered correctly more often numerically, but once we correct
for the fact that we ran ten tests, those differences are not strong enough to rule out chance.
Because the original HR survey allowed an Uncertain option and the student survey did not, the
comparison should be read as approximate rather than an exact head-to-head. Table B1 lists each
myth and per-item results.

Exhibit C: Interpreting p = .01 Across Reference Samples
Overview
Students answered the six-statement Haller and Krauss (2002) instrument on
interpretations of a statistically significant result. All six statements are incorrect. A respondent is
scored as fully correct only if all six are marked False. Class results are compared descriptively
to the Gigerenzer (2004) reference percentages. 
Comparison samples
The three reference groups come from Haller and Krauss (2002), a study conducted in
Germany and reproduced as Figure 1 in Gigerenzer (2004). They are: (a) psychology students (n
= 44), (b) university faculty in psychology departments who do not teach statistics (n = 39), and
(c) university faculty who do teach statistics (n = 30). In plain terms, these are three groups with
progressively more formal statistics training. The published finding that gets attention is that
even statistics instructors miss the question often. 
Data
Class responses came from a Canvas survey administered during the Gigerenzer exercise
(n = 18), using a True/False format for each of the six statements. Reference percentages of fully
correct respondents follow Gigerenzer (2004, Figure 1): psychology students 0 %, psychology
faculty not teaching statistics 10 %, and psychology faculty teaching statistics 20 %. 
Analysis
The overall class performance was evaluated with a one-sided exact binomial test. The
outcome was the share of students fully correct on all six items. The benchmark was the 10 %
rate reported for psychology faculty who do not teach statistics (Haller & Krauss, 2002). The
20 % rate for statistics-teaching faculty is reported for reference. Cohort, wording, and

administration differ across the original study and the course survey, so the test is meant as a

Results
Six of 18 students (33.3 %, 95 % CI [13.3 %, 59.0 %]) answered all six items correctly.
Against the 10 % benchmark for faculty who do not teach statistics, the one-sided exact binomial
test was statistically significant, p = .006. Against the stricter 20 % benchmark for statistics-
teaching faculty, the test was not significant, p = .13. In plain terms, the class clearly
outperformed the non-statistics faculty benchmark but could not be distinguished from the
statistics-teaching faculty benchmark on this small sample. Table C1 lists each of the six
