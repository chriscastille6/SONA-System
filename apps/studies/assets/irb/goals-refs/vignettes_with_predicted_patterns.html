Risk Preference (Below vs. Above Goal)
Sources: Larrick et al. (2009), John salesperson; Heath et al. (1999) Problem 1; Au & Feldman (2020) matched.
Condition A — Below goal (6 units)
Imagine you are a salesperson. Your goal for this month is to sell 10 units. So far you have sold 6 units. You have one more opportunity: you can choose either (A) a sure outcome of 2 more units, or (B) a 50% chance of 4 more units and 50% chance of 0 more units. Which would you choose? (A) Sure 2 units (B) 50% chance of 4, 50% chance of 0

Extension vignettes

Extensions use Likert 1–7; no informed priors from literature (default/weakly informative).

EXT-GS1
Sales Misreporting
Manipulation: Below vs. above; commitment; goal source; distance.
High commitment, participatively set, just shy (below)
Jordan is a salesperson. Jordan and the manager discussed and set a goal of 15 units this month. Jordan has made it a priority, and a bonus is tied to hitting it. So far Jordan has sold 14 units. Jordan has one more potential sale, but the customer is uncertain. Jordan reported the sale as "confirmed" (which would help hit the goal) even though the customer might cancel. Reporting it as confirmed is technically allowed but ethically questionable. How acceptable was Jordan's decision to report the sale as confirmed? 1 = Not at all acceptable … 7 = Completely acceptable
Low commitment, assigned, far (below)
Jordan is a salesperson. The manager assigned Jordan a goal of 15 units; Jordan doesn't particularly care about it. So far Jordan has sold 5 units. Jordan has one more potential sale, but the customer is uncertain. Jordan reported the sale as "confirmed" (which would help hit the goal) even though the customer might cancel. Reporting it as confirmed is technically allowed but ethically questionable. How acceptable was Jordan's decision to report the sale as confirmed? 1 = Not at all acceptable … 7 = Completely acceptable
Above goal (control)
Jordan is a salesperson. Jordan and the manager discussed and set a goal of 15 units. So far Jordan has sold 18 units. [Same scenario: Jordan reported the sale as confirmed.] How acceptable was Jordan's decision to report the sale as confirmed? 1 = Not at all acceptable … 7 = Completely acceptable

DV: Likert 1–7. Prior: Default. Tests: H2a; commitment × distance moderation.

EXT-GS2
Expense Inflation
Manipulation: Below vs. above; commitment; goal source; distance.
High commitment, participatively set, just shy (below)
Sam set a personal savings goal of $500 this month with a financial advisor. Sam has made it a priority. So far Sam has saved $480. Sam had a work trip. Sam claimed a slightly inflated expense (e.g., added $30 for a meal Sam didn't have) to help reach the savings goal. This is against policy but hard to detect. How acceptable was Sam's decision to inflate the expense? 1 = Not at all acceptable … 7 = Completely acceptable
Low commitment, assigned, far (below)
A savings target of $500 was assigned to Sam; Sam doesn't particularly care about it. So far Sam has saved $150. Sam had a work trip. Sam claimed a slightly inflated expense (e.g., added $30 for a meal Sam didn't have) to help reach the savings goal. This is against policy but hard to detect. How acceptable was Sam's decision to inflate the expense? 1 = Not at all acceptable … 7 = Completely acceptable
Above goal (control)
Sam set a personal savings goal of $500 this month. So far Sam has saved $600. [Same scenario: Sam inflated the expense.] How acceptable was Sam's decision to inflate the expense? 1 = Not at all acceptable … 7 = Completely acceptable

DV: Likert 1–7. Prior: Default. Tests: H2a; commitment × distance moderation.

EXT-AM1
Elastic Estimate and Goal Pressure
Manipulation: Behind vs. ahead of schedule; commitment.
Behind schedule, high commitment
Morgan is a project manager. Morgan's goal is to deliver the project "on time." The deadline is somewhat flexible—"on time" could mean the original date or up to a week later. Morgan and the client agreed on the timeline; it matters for Morgan's reputation. Morgan is behind schedule. A client asked: "Will you deliver on time?" Morgan answered (A) "Yes, we will deliver on time" (stretching the definition of "on time" if needed), rather than (B) "We are working toward the original date but may need a few extra days." How justified was Morgan's answer? 1 = Not at all justified … 7 = Completely justified
Ahead of schedule (control)
[Same setup, but "Morgan is ahead of schedule" and gave the same answer.] How justified was Morgan's answer? 1 = Not at all justified … 7 = Completely justified

DV: Likert 1–7. Prior: Default. Tests: H2b.

EXT-AM2
Numeric Claim with Elasticity
Manipulation: Below vs. above; commitment; distance.
High commitment, just shy (below)
Riley is a fundraiser. Riley set a goal with the team to raise $10,000. Riley has raised $9,200 so far. A donor asked how much Riley has raised. The exact amount is somewhat uncertain (some pledges may not materialize). Riley reported a more optimistic estimate that includes likely pledges ($9,500) rather than the conservative estimate ($9,200). How acceptable was Riley's decision to report the higher amount? 1 = Not at all acceptable … 7 = Completely acceptable
Low commitment, far (below)
Riley is a fundraiser. A goal of $10,000 was assigned to Riley; Riley doesn't particularly care. Riley has raised $4,000 so far. [Same scenario: Riley reported the higher amount.] How acceptable was Riley's decision to report the higher amount? 1 = Not at all acceptable … 7 = Completely acceptable

DV: Likert 1–7. Prior: Default. Tests: H2b.

EXT-AM3
Attribution (Goals Caused Unethical Behavior)
Manipulation: Commitment; goal source; below vs. above.
High commitment, participatively set, below goal
Alex is a salesperson. Alex and the manager discussed and set a goal of 20 units. Alex cared about hitting it. Alex sold 12 units and then misreported a borderline sale as confirmed to reach the goal. To what extent do you think the goal caused Alex's behavior? 1 = Not at all … 7 = Completely
Low commitment, assigned, below goal
Alex is a salesperson. A goal of 20 units was assigned to Alex; Alex didn't particularly care. Alex sold 12 units and then misreported a borderline sale as confirmed. To what extent do you think the goal caused Alex's behavior? 1 = Not at all … 7 = Completely
Above goal (control)
Alex is a salesperson with a goal of 20 units. Alex sold 22 units and then misreported a borderline sale as confirmed (for other reasons). To what extent do you think the goal caused Alex's behavior? 1 = Not at all … 7 = Completely

DV: Likert 1–7. Prior: Default. Tests: H2c; commitment moderation.

EXT-GS4
Goal Legitimacy
Manipulation: Legitimate vs. illegitimate goal (procedural fairness of goal-setting).
Legitimate goal (below)
Imagine you are a salesperson. Your goal of 12 units this month was set through a fair process—you and your manager reviewed benchmarks, discussed what was achievable, and agreed on the target. So far you have sold 11 units. You have one more potential sale, but the customer is uncertain. You can choose: (A) Report the sale as "confirmed" (helps hit the goal; customer might cancel), or (B) Report it as uncertain. Which would you choose? (A) Report as confirmed (B) Report as uncertain
Illegitimate goal (below)
Imagine you are a salesperson. Your goal of 12 units this month was imposed by headquarters without your input—it doesn't match industry benchmarks, and your team widely views it as unfair. So far you have sold 11 units. You have one more potential sale, but the customer is uncertain. You can choose: (A) Report the sale as "confirmed" (helps hit the goal; customer might cancel), or (B) Report it as uncertain. Which would you choose? (A) Report as confirmed (B) Report as uncertain

DV: Binary (A vs B). Prior: Default. Tests: Goal legitimacy.

EXT-AM4
Amoral Manipulation × Resource Context (Behavioral Expectations)
Manipulation: Character described as high vs. low on amoral manipulation (MACH tactics facet); organizational context as scarcity culture + zero-sum vs. abundance culture + commitment to reallocating/sharing. DV: Likelihood of task-focused vs. undermining behaviors.
Low amoral manipulation — Abundance culture
Alex works in an organization whose culture is that resources exist but are often hidden or underused and can be unlocked—and that the organization is committed to reallocating or sharing resources so that success depends on people cooperating and pooling effort. Colleagues describe Alex as direct and straightforward—someone who doesn't play politics, avoids flattering others or cutting corners to get ahead, and believes in being transparent about the real reasons for doing things. How likely is it that Alex would do each of the following? (1 = Extremely unlikely … 7 = Extremely likely) • Put in extra effort on their own deliverables (Task performance) • Share information with teammates to improve joint outcomes (OCB) • Let errors go uncorrected when it suits them (Production deviance) • Withhold useful information from peers (Social undermining) • Spread negative information about a colleague to others (Social undermining) • Deliberately set traps or create situations so that a highly effective coworker would fail or look bad (Sabotage) • Receive a favorable performance appraisal from their supervisor (Job performance) (1 = Extremely unlikely … 7 = Extremely likely)
High amoral manipulation — Abundance culture
Alex works in an organization whose culture is that resources exist but are often hidden or underused and can be unlocked—and that the organization is committed to reallocating or sharing resources so that success depends on people cooperating and pooling effort. Colleagues describe Alex as someone who believes the best way to handle people is to tell them what they want to hear; Alex is willing to flatter others and cut corners when it helps get ahead, and doesn't think you need to reveal the real reason for doing something unless it's useful. Low amoral manipulation — Scarcity culture
Alex works in an organization whose culture is that there aren't enough resources to do the right thing, and that view is used to justify short-term or wasteful decisions; resources and rewards are limited (e.g., only one person can get the promotion, or the bonus pool is fixed so one person's gain is another's loss). High amoral manipulation — Scarcity culture
Alex works in an organization whose culture is that there aren't enough resources to do the right thing, and that view is used to justify short-term or wasteful decisions; resources and rewards are limited (e.g., only one person can get the promotion, or the bonus pool is fixed so one person's gain is another's loss). DV: Likert 1–7 likelihood per item. Constructs: Task performance (extra effort); OCB (share information); Production deviance (Spector: let errors go uncorrected); Social undermining (withhold, spread negative); Sabotage (set traps so highly effective coworker fails or looks bad); Job performance (supervisor appraisal). Prior: Default. Hypothesis: Amoral manipulation seen as more functional in scarcity (zero-sum) environments → interaction such that high amoral × scarcity culture yields higher expected undermining, sabotage (target = highly effective coworker), and possibly lower expected task focus than high amoral × abundance culture.
