Scientific Purpose and Objectives

This study tests whether goals function as reference points such that being below a goal increases risk-seeking and acceptability of ethically questionable strategies.

  1. Replicate classic goal-framing effects on risky choice.
  2. Extend goal-framing effects to ethical acceptability judgments.
  3. Compare below-goal versus above-goal conditions across vignette types.
  4. Contribute evidence on goals, reference points, and ethical decision-making.

Full Participant Flow

  1. Consent: Electronic informed consent before any study item.
  2. Demographics and Background: Brief demographics module when needed for analyses.
  3. Vignette Assignment (Wave 1): All four vignettes in the pool below (R1, R2, E1, E2); random order; one goal frame (below or above) per vignette.
  4. Response Format: Binary risky-choice items (replication) and/or 1-7 acceptability ratings (extension).
  5. Attention Checks: One to two quality-check items.
  6. Optional Follow-up: Optional email for findings summary (stored separately if provided).
  7. Debrief: Study purpose/hypothesis explanation and contact information.

Recruitment Listing Text (Reviewer Copy)

Study Title: Goals as Reference Points and Risk Taking

Description: This study examines how goals influence decision-making and risk-taking. You will complete four short scenarios in which you either make choices (sure vs. risky option) or rate how acceptable another person's behavior is.

Duration: Approximately 15-25 minutes

Mode: Online

Eligibility: Must be 18 years of age or older

Full Vignette Pool

Wave 1: Participants complete all four vignettes below (one goal frame each).

Vignette R1 (Replication) Heath et al. (1999); Larrick et al. (2009)

Below-goal frame: You have a goal of selling 10 units this month. You have sold 8 units so far (below goal). You have one more opportunity to make a sale.

Above-goal frame: You have a goal of selling 10 units this month. You have sold 12 units so far (above goal). You have one more opportunity to make a sale.

Option A: Accept a sure outcome: 1 additional unit sold (total 9 below / 13 above).

Option B: Take a risk: 50% chance of 2 units sold, 50% chance of 0 units (total 8 or 10 below / 12 or 14 above).

Vignette R2 (Replication) Au and Feldman (2020)

Below-goal frame: You need to reach a fundraising goal of $5,000. You currently have $4,200 (below goal). One donor is willing to give a guaranteed $300.

Above-goal frame: You have exceeded your fundraising goal of $5,000 and currently have $5,400. One donor is willing to give a guaranteed $300.

Option A: Accept the guaranteed $300.

Option B: Ask the donor to flip a coin: heads they give $600, tails they give $0.

Vignette E1 (Extension) Novel extension

Below-goal frame: Alex is a salesperson who needed to hit a quarterly goal to keep a bonus. With one day left, Alex was $200 short. Alex submitted an expense report that included $250 in personal meals as "client entertainment."

Above-goal frame: Alex is a salesperson who had already exceeded the quarterly goal and secured the bonus. Alex submitted an expense report that included $250 in personal meals as "client entertainment."

Prompt: How acceptable was Alex's decision to submit this expense report?

Scale: 1 = Not at all acceptable ... 7 = Completely acceptable

Vignette E2 (Extension) Novel extension

Below-goal frame: Jordan is a manager who was below the department's safety-incident goal for the year. A minor incident occurred that was not reported to central safety. Jordan did not add it to the monthly report.

Above-goal frame: Jordan is a manager whose department had already met the safety-incident goal for the year. A minor incident occurred that was not reported to central safety. Jordan did not add it to the monthly report.

Prompt: How acceptable was Jordan's decision not to include the incident in the report?

Scale: 1 = Not at all acceptable ... 7 = Completely acceptable
